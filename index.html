EXPLORE

From Indonesia to Hawaiʻi, President Obama's ties to the Asia-Pacific have shaped his values and his faith in the emerging generation of changemakers across the region. Established in 2019, the Leaders Asia-Pacific program equips emerging leaders with a values-based leadership framework—inspired by President and Mrs. Obama's legacy—to advance inclusive and lasting change in their communities. The program unites leaders from Southeast Asia, Northeast Asia, and the Pacific for leadership development and skill-building workshops focused on social change. To date, we've built a network of 340 Obama Leaders from 38 countries and territories, working across sectors and issue areas.

The Context

Change Happens in Moments

And those moments reward leaders who can bridge across divides and move communities forward.

Forces Beyond Any Single Leader's Control

Across the Asia-Pacific, the forces shaping whether societies become more inclusive or more divided are largely outside any single leader's control: geopolitics, economic pressures, technological disruption, demographic shifts. These forces create moments of flux—elections, crises, movements, institutional transitions—where the direction of change is genuinely contested.

Build Trust Through Incremental Change

Around the world, we're witnessing increasing fragmentation, polarization, and declining trust in institutions and across communities. Against this backdrop, the work doesn't wait for big moments. Leaders can drive incremental change now—fostering collaboration, creating shared experiences, building common ground across difference. This everyday work strengthens pluralism and builds the trust needed to act collectively when it matters most.

From Trust to Transformative Action

When windows for systems-level change open, trust built over time becomes the foundation for collective action. Leaders with relationships across divides can convene, align, and move communities from hope to action — shifting systems, rebuilding institutions, and expanding possibilities none could achieve alone.

Our Theory

We Invest in Leadership Readiness

We identify emerging leaders across the Asia-Pacific who are already practicing values-driven leadership—and we equip them with frameworks, skills, and a regional network so they're prepared to drive inclusive, incremental change now and mobilize collective power when transformational moments arise.

Incremental Change

Foster collaboration, build shared experiences and trust across difference — strengthening pluralism day by day.

Ready for Moments

When windows for systems-level change open, leverage that trust and mobilize collective power to drive transformation none could achieve alone.

Stronger Together

All become part of a regional community to call upon — for wisdom, solidarity, and support from peers who understand the work. None of us can do this alone.

We believe this because history shows it. Most progress is incremental — better is good, as President Obama reminds us. But history also shows that moments for transformational change can arrive faster than anyone expects. The leaders who shape what comes next are those who invested in relationships and built trust before the moment arrived.

The Leadership Gap

What's Missing—And What We Address

We've identified three interconnected gaps that prevent leaders from driving incremental change and seizing transformational moments. Our program objectives directly address each one.

1

The Bridging Gap

The leaders we select are already deeply values-driven. But values held too tightly can limit a leader's willingness to stretch, compromise, and find common ground. We help leaders reground in their core values while expanding their capacity for pragmatism, seeing values not as purity tests but as foundations for bridging across difference and building trust with unlikely allies.

Our Response → Objective 1: Ground Leaders define a values-based foundation for sustained leadership
2

The Trust Gap

The leaders we select already foster collaboration within their communities and organizations. They're looking for pathways to the harder work: building trust and common ground among people who don't naturally trust, like, or agree with each other.

Our Response → Objective 2: Collaborate Leaders cultivate relationships across difference to drive inclusive, lasting change
3

The Transformation Gap

The leaders we select are already driving change in their contexts. They're looking for frameworks to see larger systems, recognize when windows for transformational change open, and mobilize the trust and relationships they've built to move a collective from hope to action when moments arrive.

Our Response → Objective 3: Transform Leaders prepare to mobilize collective power for systems-level change

2023 Design Pivot

In 2023, President Obama elevated pluralism as a key pillar for defending democracy worldwide. Given that many Obama Leaders operate in non-democratic contexts—and amid rising global polarization—the theme resonated deeply with our international audience. Additionally, alumni raised concerns that an increasingly democracy-branded program posed safety risks, noting they had joined a leadership development program before democracy became a central focus. This inspired a curriculum pivot: reframing the program around pluralistic leadership as a means of building democratic culture, rather than centering democracy itself.

Who This Program Is For

The Leaders We Select

Our theory only works if we select the right leaders. Selection isn't administrative—it's foundational to everything that follows.

Eligibility Requirements

Location

Residing in one of 44 eligible Asia-Pacific countries and territories in Northeast Asia, Southeast Asia, and Oceania. See full list

2022 Design Pivot

We expanded eligibility to include more people who call the region home—such as refugees, asylum seekers, and non-citizens who have resided in the region for at least five years, regardless of legal status.

Geographic Focus

Work is focused on creating positive change in the Asia-Pacific region (as defined above)

Age & Experience

24-45 years old with at least 3 years in a leadership role

2023 Design Pivot

We increased the age limit from 40 to 45 across Obama Leaders programs. A research review revealed we were likely missing leaders who embarked on their leadership journey later in life—particularly working mothers, Indigenous women, and women of color who historically face more barriers reaching leadership positions. Since making this change, Leaders joining between ages 40-45 have indeed tended to be women of color, working mothers, and Indigenous leaders.

Commitment

8-12 hours per month for 6 months, primarily Saturday mornings

Language

Professionally proficient in English

2024-2025 Design Pivot

For the language requirement, we moved from "written and spoken proficiency" to "professionally proficient" to use more inclusive language.

No Active Campaigns

Elected officials are welcome, but participants cannot be campaigning for office during the program term (501c3 requirement)

Intentional Cohort of 35 Composition

We deliberately build cohorts that mix:

Cross-Sector Leaders

Public sector, private sector, and civil society—including leaders rebuilding trust from within institutions alongside those pushing for change from outside

Age Range & Generational Perspectives

Leaders aged 24-45, enabling peer mentorship across career stages and bringing together different generational perspectives on change

Geographic Diversity

Each cohort typically represents 20-25 countries and territories across Northeast Asia, Southeast Asia, and Oceania

Issues & Disciplines

Climate, education, health, security and defense, AI, human rights, arts, policy, community organizing, and more—ensuring cross-pollination of approaches

92% of Asia-Pacific Leaders agreed their cohort included a group of leaders whose ideological perspectives varied and challenged and/or sharpened their own thinking.

2022 Design Pivot

Diversity is the curriculum. Leaders learn pluralism by practicing it. A cohort spanning 38 countries, multiple sectors, and diverse issue areas means every session is an exercise in engaging across difference—not as theory, but as lived experience.

The cohort as practice ground. We teach leaders to bridge across difference, build trust with those unlike them, and find common ground amid divergent perspectives. The cohort itself becomes the space to practice these skills—making the learning embodied, not abstract.

Network effects multiply impact. When a Filipino education leader knows Thai climate activists, Indonesian civic technologists, and Australian community organizers, the potential for collaboration expands exponentially. The cohort becomes infrastructure for future action.

Program Architecture

Our pedagogy draws from multiple traditions to develop values-based leaders:

Dialogic (Bakhtin, Freire, Vygotsky)
Understanding emerges where "two or more voices come into contact" — knowledge is co-constructed through mutual exchange rather than transmitted from knower to learner

Dialectic (Hegel, Socrates)
Understanding emerges through the productive confrontation of opposing ideas; contradictions and tensions are examined until they give rise to new, more complete insights

Transformative (Mezirow)
Learning that fundamentally shifts how leaders see themselves and the world — triggered by critical reflection on assumptions and "disorienting dilemmas" that challenge existing worldviews

Narrative (Storytelling-based)
Knowledge constructed through story — personal narrative as a tool for self-understanding, identity formation, and leadership development

Contemplative (Reflective practice)
Inner work through silence, journaling, and reflection — creating space for integration, meaning-making, and values clarification

Community of Practice (Lave & Wenger)
Learning as participation in a community with shared practices — identity formation through belonging; the cohort becomes infrastructure for growth

Why this sequence?

The modules build intentionally: Leaders must first understand their own power and values (Power) before they can genuinely engage across difference (Polarities). Only then can they move from dialogue to collaborative action (Pragmatism), and finally develop the resilience to sustain pluralistic leadership over time (Peacebuilding). Each stage creates the foundation for the next.

Why weekly for 5 months?

Ritual builds community. Weekly sessions create rhythm, familiarity, and repeated contact that turns strangers into trusted peers. By session 20, they're a community—not just a cohort.

Transformation requires sustained engagement. Mindset shifts don't happen in a weekend. The 5-month arc gives leaders time to encounter new frameworks, try them in their real work, reflect, and try again.

Why Saturdays at 10am Singapore?

Spanning 19 time zones. Our cohorts include leaders from Hawai'i (UTC-10) to Kiribati (UTC+14). 10am Singapore allows Hawai'i to join at 4:00pm Friday.

Inclusion of working adults and parents. Saturday mornings allow participation without sacrificing careers or family responsibilities.

Note: DC-based staff run sessions 10:00 PM–1:00 AM ET. Pilots at 4:00 PM Singapore saw attendance drop from ~90% to ~65%.

Dialogic Community of Practice

Community Groups

Monthly · 1 hour · 5-6 Leaders per group

Meaning-making and practice with peers

  • Create a sense of belonging
  • Integrate learning in context
  • Practice pluralism and navigate tension
  • Bear witness to one another's growth
Why small groups of 5-6?

The small group as the unit of belonging. Inspired by Peter Block's work on community, deep trust and vulnerability require intimate settings. Full cohort sessions of 35 can inspire and teach—but belonging happens in circles of 5-6.

Alumni-facilitated. Community Groups are facilitated by program alumni who model the leadership we cultivate, providing space for honest conversation that transforms how leaders see themselves and each other.

Individual Coaching

Monthly · 1 hour

Personal understanding and contextual application

  • Deepen self-awareness and values-based leadership
  • Strengthen adaptive and relational capacity
  • Build resilience for the long arc of leadership
Why 1:1 coaching?

Individualized reflection space. Group sessions can't provide the personal attention each leader needs. Coaching offers dedicated space to process learnings, connect frameworks to personal context, and work through specific challenges.

Depth requires dedicated coaches. Foundation staff cannot provide this level of support to 35 leaders simultaneously. Dedicated coaches ensure sustained, personal attention throughout the program.

In-Person Convening

Community of Practice Transformative

1 week · End of program · Asia-Pacific location

Culminating experience that transforms digital relationships into embodied ones—the kind of connection that sustains across years and distance.

Why in-person at the end?

Culmination, not introduction. Many programs start with an intensive then struggle to maintain momentum. We invert this: the in-person week is earned, anticipated, and serves as a capstone that cements community.

Respecting leaders' constraints. Asking leaders to take a week away is significant. Placing it at the end—once trust is built and value demonstrated—honors that sacrifice.

Honest Reflection

What We're Learning

After 5 cohorts and 340 leaders, here's where we are in our understanding — what we're confident about, what we're actively testing, and what remains uncertain.

What We're Confident About

Evidence supports these elements of our theory:

The Hope to Action® framework shifts leadership capacities.

All 4 cohorts show 100% progress on program objectives. Pre/post data shows consistent gains: +39% in understanding values-based leadership, +29% in network strength, +24% in ability to motivate collective action, +22% in confidence as change agents.

Our curriculum redesign is working.

After the 2022-2023 curriculum overhaul integrating pluralism throughout the Hope to Action framework, session satisfaction jumped +5.3 percentage points (from 85% to 90%). Top-rated sessions across years: Public Narrative (92-95%) and Storytelling Night (91-95%).

1:1 Coaching is highly effective and consistent.

Coaching satisfaction remains high across cohorts (90-96%) and coaching partners. Leaders consistently cite it as one of the most valuable program elements. 100% completion rate in all cohorts to date.

We're attracting the right leaders.

CliftonStrengths data across 140 leaders shows consistent profiles—Learners, Achievers, Connectors with low Influencing—aligned with our theory of pluralistic leadership. 92% report their cohort's ideological diversity challenged and sharpened their thinking.

Virtual delivery builds real connection across 19 time zones.

Network strength gains (+29% avg) and 97% intention to stay engaged as alumni demonstrate that 5 months of virtual engagement creates meaningful, lasting bonds. Saturday 10am Singapore timing maintains ~85% attendance.

Optional supports add value for those who use them.

Staff 1:1s (94% → 98% satisfaction) and Strategy Advisor 1:1s (89% → 93% satisfaction) show consistent high ratings, indicating these optional offerings meaningfully support leaders who opt in.

What We're Actively Testing

Elements we're iterating on and watching closely:

AI-enabled diagnostic tools for pre-session learning.

We're piloting vibe-coded assessments that let leaders explore concepts (e.g., types of power) before sessions. Leaders complete a 10-20 minute assessment, see their personalized results, and arrive ready to engage—cutting approximately 1 hour of content delivery from session time and shifting toward more time for peer-learning and discussion. Two examples include our Power Perception Tool and Enemyfying Assessment.

Alumni facilitation of Community Groups.

In 2024-2025, we shifted to alumni facilitators to model the leadership we cultivate. Early results are promising: satisfaction jumped from 84% to 92% (+8 points). We're continuing to monitor whether this improvement holds and whether it produces better belonging and peer connection outcomes.

Effectiveness of Community Groups overall.

We believe small groups of 5-6 are the unit of belonging (per Peter Block), and the data is improving. But Community Groups have historically been our lowest-rated pillar. We're still learning how to optimize facilitation quality, session design, and frequency for maximum impact.

Understanding how coaching complements the core curriculum.

Coaches report thematic summaries after each cohort, revealing consistent patterns: leaders work through tensions around identity and authenticity, managing polarities (work/life, speaking out/staying silent, tradition/innovation), building resilience against burnout, and boundary work with stakeholders. We see clear alignment between coaching conversations and curriculum themes—but need more data to understand precisely how individual coaching deepens or extends what's introduced in sessions.

What We're Still Uncertain About

Open questions we don't yet have answers to:

Which leadership moment benefits most from the program.

Our 2025-2026 cohort skews toward Building (40%) and Transitioning (37%), with fewer in Starting (0%), Sustaining (11%), or Scaling (11%). We don't yet know which stage sees the greatest transformation. Longitudinal analysis planned as we collect more cohort data.

Long-term impact on pluralistic culture in the region.

Our theory rests on readiness: prepared leaders will act when windows open. But tracking systemic impact across 38 countries over years is methodologically challenging. We're investing in post-program tracking but acknowledge this remains our biggest open question.

Optimal balance of pedagogical approaches.

Our program draws on six pedagogical traditions (dialogic, dialectic, transformative, narrative, contemplative, community of practice). We have strong intuition about which approaches serve which program elements—but we haven't yet isolated which combinations drive the strongest outcomes.
